Assembly member Daniel O’Donnell criticizes Columbia for ‘failure’ to improve accessibility at 125th Street subway station

O’Donnell’s statement comes amid a yearslong push for the University to build an elevator at the station.

By Uriel Lopez

Graphic by Molly Durawa

February 29, 2024

Judy Goldstein / Staff Photographer

Daniel O’Donnell, District 69 New York State Assembly member, released a statement on Thursday criticizing Columbia for its “continual failure to fulfill its moral obligation to the West Harlem and Morningside Heights communities” and urged the University to install an elevator at the subway station on Broadway and West 125th Street.

The MTA’s 1 train lacks ADA-accessible stations between 96th Street on the Upper West Side to Dyckman Street in Inwood. Currently the station, which is one of the highest above-ground stations in the city, has escalators but no elevator.

Under the West Harlem Community Benefits Agreements, Columbia must provide certain benefits, including the “widening of the escalators to improve station accessibility,” O’Donnell’s statement reads. There have yet to be any modifications started or announced at the property.

“All these institutions that surround my home, all have these ideas that they want to go bigger, broader, wider, taller, whatever else it is,” O’Donnell said of Columbia’s Manhattanville expansion in an interview with Spectator. “And they don’t always fully grasp that those ideas can have a very strong negative impact on the people who live here.”

The Manhattanville campus, located directly under the 125th Street station, was announced in 2003. In 2009, due to the large-scale displacement necessary to build the campus under eminent domain, the CBA was arranged between Columbia and West Harlem representatives. For years, however, many residents and community leaders have said that Columbia hasn’t lived up to its promises.

“All the people who will need to use the 125th Street subway station to get to the Manhattanville campus need better transportation models,” O’Donnell said. “They need to accommodate their workers, teachers, students who need ADA compliance. So in order to do all that, they got to kick in.”

Located at the border of Manhattanville and Morningside Heights, 125th Street station provides service north to Van Cortlandt Street in the Bronx and south to South Ferry in downtown Manhattan. The site has two escalator entrances on either side of Broadway, designed with steps to accommodate one person at a time. While many have called on Columbia to help install an elevator at the station, Columbia has only committed to help widen the escalators.

“Columbia is committed to replacing and widening the two street-to-mezzanine escalators on the station’s west side, a major capital upgrade that will improve the daily experience of everyone using the station,” University spokesperson Ben Chang wrote in a statement. “The planning and design of this project has begun, and we will continue to coordinate this work with the MTA to ensure that our upgrades are delivered as quickly as possible and with minimal disruption to the day-to-day operations of the station.”

While the University has hired an engineering team to study the station, public officials including O’Donnell say they have yet to see the report and that it is not publicly available.

Following a city lawsuit last year, the MTA is required by law to complete the construction of ramps or elevators at 95 percent of stations by 2055 to meet ADA compliance. The MTA settlement states that subway stations must be fitted with elevators or ramps to accommodate those with mobility impairments.

“Additional enhancements are needed to ensure the longer-term safety and accessibility of the 125th Street Station and that is why we have been working alongside local elected officials, community groups and others to continue advocating for public investments to support these critical upgrades,” Chang wrote in the statement. “In recent months, we have made significant progress in our work with the MTA toward these shared goals. We remain deeply committed to this process and will continue to work alongside local stakeholders to support the MTA in delivering these essential upgrades and in maintaining a public asset in our community that is welcoming, safe, and accessible to all.”

The MTA did not respond to requests for comment.

John Burdeaux, who frequently commutes from the 125th Street station, said he has noticed the lack of an elevator or other accessible services.

“Sometimes when my hip is bothering me,” Burdeaux said. “We do have the escalators, but you know, you still have to walk up.”

While issues of space affect the MTA’s ability to construct structures like elevators at every train station, O’Donnell said 125th Street does not fall into this category. In his statement last week, O’Donnell included a link to a petition he created in 2022 urging the University to comply with the CBA and implement an elevator at the site. In the petition, he noted that the station is currently difficult to access for “people with disabilities, seniors, individuals carrying children or using strollers, and others.”

As of late February, the petition has over 500 signatures, and is supported by New York’s 13th District Representative Adriano Espaillat, Manhattan Borough President Mark Levine, City Council member Shaun Abreu, CC ’14, as well as the Morningside Heights Community Coalition, WE ACT for Environmental Justice, and Manhattan Community Board 9.

“If Columbia wants to keep on building, building, building, and putting more offices, and putting more labs, and putting more classrooms, the people who they expect to live there or to get there have to be able to get there, and they need to participate in making that happen,” O’Donnell said.
